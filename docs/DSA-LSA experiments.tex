The model is used to study two permanent fiscal shocks under two financing regimes. The shocks are a permanent increase in government consumption $G_t$ and a permanent increase in public investment $I_t^g$, each of size $\Delta = 0.02\,\bar{Y}$, where $\bar{Y}$ is mean baseline output. Two financing regimes are compared against a no-shock baseline: (i) debt-financed, where debt $B_t$ accumulates as the residual of equation~\eqref{eq:debt} with no policy adjustment; and (ii) labour-tax-financed, where $\tau^l_t$ rises by a constant $\Delta\tau^l$ found by bisection.

\subsection{Experiment Design}

\paragraph{Baseline.} All policy paths are constant at the values in Table~\ref{tab:params}. The interest rate is fixed at $r = 0.04$. Government consumption is calibrated to $G^{base} = 0.30\,\bar{Y}$. Public investment sustains the steady-state public capital stock: $I_t^{g,base} = \delta_g K_0^g$. Initial debt is $B_0 = 0$.

\paragraph{Shocks.} The permanent shocks are:
\begin{align}
G\text{ shock:} \quad & G_t^{cf} = G^{base} + \Delta, \label{eq:G-shock}\\
I^g\text{ shock:} \quad & I_t^{g,cf} = I^{g,base} + \Delta. \label{eq:Ig-shock}
\end{align}
The $I^g$ shock accumulates in public capital through \eqref{eq:kg}, raising output via \eqref{eq:production} and wages via \eqref{eq:foc-w}.

\paragraph{Financing regimes.} Under \emph{debt financing}, tax rates are unchanged and debt accumulates through \eqref{eq:debt}. Under \emph{labour-tax financing}, a constant $\Delta\tau^l$ is added to the baseline labour tax path and found by bisection to satisfy the terminal flow-balance condition at horizon $T_{trans} = 40$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:balance-condition}
\frac{\text{PD}_{T_{trans}-1}}{Y_{T_{trans}-1}} = (g - r)\,b^*,
\end{equation}
where $b^* = B_0/Y_0$ is the target debt-to-GDP ratio. The right-hand side is the primary surplus required to stabilise debt at $b^*$ when the growth-adjusted interest rate is $r - g > 0$. In the test configuration $B_0 = 0$, so $b^* = 0$ and the condition reduces to primary balance. For calibrated runs with $B_0 > 0$ the target is a surplus of $(r-g)\,b^*$. Agents have perfect foresight over all paths. Results extend $n_{post} = 20$ periods beyond $T_{trans}$ to show post-target dynamics.

\paragraph{Fiscal multiplier.} For the debt-financed scenario, the period-by-period multiplier is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}_t = \frac{Y_t^{cf} - Y_t^{base}}{\Delta}.
\end{equation}

Under debt financing, the primary deficit rises one-for-one with $\Delta G$ on impact. Since $r_t$ is exogenous, the firm's first-order condition \eqref{eq:foc-k} fixes $K_t/L_t$ independently of the shock, so output and wages do not change and the fiscal multiplier is approximately zero. Debt-to-GDP grows without bound. Under labour-tax financing, a permanent $\Delta\tau^l$ satisfies condition~\eqref{eq:balance-condition} at period $T_{trans}$. The higher labour tax reduces after-tax wages, compressing labour supply and consumption. Pension and UI expenditure respond through general equilibrium effects on the cross-sectional distribution.

The $I^g$ shock differs from the $G$ shock through the public capital channel. As $K_t^g$ accumulates through \eqref{eq:kg}, output rises for any given $K_t/L_t$ via \eqref{eq:production}. The wage rises through \eqref{eq:foc-w}, expanding household income and the tax base. The fiscal multiplier is positive and grows over time as public capital builds up. Under labour-tax financing, the $\Delta\tau^l$ satisfying~\eqref{eq:balance-condition} is smaller than for the $G$ shock because the expanding tax base partially offsets the cost of the investment.
